Walk into any consulting firm and ask what their target utilization rate is. You will hear a number between 75% and 90%, delivered with the confidence of a physical constant. Partners will tell you it is the single metric they watch most closely. Ops leads will tell you it is the number the board cares about. Everyone will tell you higher is better, until it isn't.

The paradox is that past a certain threshold — typically somewhere between 78% and 85%, depending on the firm's delivery model — **higher utilization actively destroys margin**. Not margin per hour. Total margin. And the firms chasing 90% utilization are usually the ones with the worst profitability, the highest turnover, and the thinnest pipeline.

## The math of over-utilization

Here is the simplified version. A consultant billing at $300/hr and running at 70% utilization generates $420k/year of revenue (70% × 2,000 hrs × $300). Push them to 90% and naively you get $540k/year — a 29% revenue bump, with no new salary cost. Partners see this math and push.

What the math doesn't capture:

- **Scoping quality drops.** Senior people with no slack write worse proposals, because they cannot think about the next engagement while drowning in the current one. The firm's win rate declines. Estimation accuracy declines.
- **Delivery quality drops.** Over-utilized teams cut corners. Overrun rates rise, client satisfaction drops, references dry up.
- **Learning stops.** Post-engagement retros get skipped. Institutional memory degrades. The next engagement rebuilds context from zero.
- **Retention collapses.** The firm's best people leave first, because they have the most options. Replacing them costs 1.5–2× annual salary.

When you net these out, most firms running at 88–90% utilization are worse off than the same firm at 75%.

## The threshold effect

The key insight is that the relationship between utilization and margin is not linear. It is a curve that rises, then falls off a cliff. The cliff is different for every firm, but it exists.

Below the cliff, every point of utilization adds margin. Above it, every point of utilization subtracts margin — through lost deals, overruns, churn, and learning decay. Partners who think in straight lines ("more hours = more revenue") never see the cliff until they're over it.

## How to find your own threshold

The cliff is firm-specific, but three signals help locate it:

1. **Proposal quality**: are your best people writing proposals in 4 hours now when they used to take 12? If yes, quality has degraded even if the deal volume looks fine.
2. **Overrun rate on the newest senior hires**: if consultants hired in the last 12 months are overrunning more than their senior peers, the firm is not making time for them to absorb context. That is a utilization problem disguised as an onboarding problem.
3. **Time between retro and next engagement of the same type**: if retros are happening weeks after the engagement closes (or not at all), learning is not feeding the next sale.

## The fix is not lower utilization

The answer is not to tell partners "aim for 72% instead of 85%." That lasts one quarter. The answer is to build the connection between delivery and scoping so tight that the senior people do not have to manually re-absorb context on every new engagement.

When a firm runs on a [Professional Services OS](/help/glossary/what-is-a-professional-services-os/), scoping a new engagement starts from the closest completed one, with assumptions and risks pre-populated. The effective cost of writing a proposal drops. Senior people can be 85% utilized without losing quality, because the institutional memory is in the system, not in their heads.

Utilization is a symptom. The disease is that services firms expect their most expensive people to act as human integration layers between scoping and delivery. Take that burden off, and the cliff moves.
